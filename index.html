02 Architecture

Three diagrams — topology, multi-link failover, and QoS traffic prioritization — explained step by step.

Spoke-Hub Topology
  1. Every branch edge tunnels back to one place — the data center hub — instead of meshing directly with every other branch. That keeps site-to-site routing simple: one control point, not thousands of peer relationships.
  2. vManage sits at the hub as the control plane. It pushes policy — routing, QoS, security — to every branch edge, so a rule change is made once centrally, not 4,000 times manually.
  3. Each spoke carries its own set of physical links underneath — that's what the next diagram breaks down.
Multi-Link Failover Order
active link
failover trigger
standby link
  1. MPLS is primary — carries traffic under normal conditions because it's the most stable, lowest-latency path.
  2. If MPLS goes down, the edge fails over to ILL — a leased-line backup, next in reliability.
  3. If ILL is also unavailable, 4G takes over — wireless, higher latency, but keeps critical traffic moving.
  4. PPPoE is the next fallback, used when the broadband/DSL path is the only thing left standing.
  5. RF is the last resort — brought in only when every wired and cellular option has failed, to avoid a full site outage.
QoS — Application-Aware Traffic Prioritization
  1. All traffic hits the QoS engine first, where it's classified by application — not just by port or protocol, but by what the traffic actually is.
  2. Voice and video get the high-priority queue — they're the most sensitive to delay and jitter, so they're protected first.
  3. Core business applications sit in a medium queue — protected from congestion, but behind real-time traffic.
  4. Everything else — bulk transfers, general browsing — goes into best-effort and absorbs the congestion when a link is under load, instead of critical traffic being the one that suffers.
